Problem 1 – Owners Now Pay Double for Buildings ×

Published escalation data since 1967 shows that today's buildings cost more than twice what national inflation would justify. Some owners are paying nearly three times what they should. And there's no sign of the trend slowing.

Figure 1 illustrates how construction costs began diverging from national inflation in the 1960s, with major spikes around 1980, 2004, and a sustained rise since 2011. COVID added another layer of volatility, pushing prices even higher.

Figure 1 – Construction Escalation vs. National Inflation

This trend isn't anecdotal—it's backed by transparent, nationally published data. While many builders and designers have ignored or buried these insights, they also stand to benefit most by helping reverse the trend.

Data Sources Anyone Can Verify:

  • National Inflation – U.S. Consumer Price Index data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics, accessible via US Inflation Calculator.
  • Construction Cost Escalation – Ed Zarenski's Construction Analytics, which compiles building cost indices from industry sources dating back to 1967.

Figure 2 – Construction Building Cost Indices from edzarenski.com

Even before COVID, costs were already exceeding justifiable levels. As we'll explore further in Problem 4: Cost-Based Compensation, misaligned incentives and risk-averse budgeting have fueled a runaway trend that data—and better delivery models—can now help solve.

Problem 2 - Exponential Growth in Waste×

Since 1967, indirect labor, overhead, and other waste has grown 4.5 times what national inflation justifies. In 1967 overhead and profit was about a quarter of what the owner paid. In 2025 it is well over half. The data shows that the problem is not due to materials or labor—it's systemic waste built into how buildings are designed and contracted for.

Figure 1 – The Systemic Problem Over Time

Let's take an example of a $5M project in 1967. Based on national inflation that building should cost $47.7M today. But, as the Construction Analytics data shows, owners now pay $96.1M—more than double what inflation warrants.

Problem 3 – Fragmented and Archaic Processes ×

Despite decades of technological advancement—BIM, VDC, IPD, VR, and more—construction planning and preconstruction remain fragmented, manual, and disconnected. Unlike manufacturing or retail, where integrated systems drive automation and efficiency, building projects are still driven by spreadsheets, siloed tools, and tribal knowledge.

Nearly every planning and preconstruction process—from business case development to estimating and cost management—requires manual, file-based transactions between owners, designers, and builders. These handoffs are not only inefficient, they are fundamentally incompatible with the needs of complex building programs.

Figure 1 shows the reality:
Thirteen core processes are split across disconnected roles and tools. Owners, owner reps, architects, and builders each operate in isolation, using different and inconsistent information and data standards and structures.

Figure 1 – Planning and Preconstruction Silos

Even within organizations—especially builders—there's little to no integration between departments like business development, estimating, procurement, project management, or accounting. The result is that as new technologies are applied - they only expand the overhead within each silo. This further entrenches the prevention of any form of measurable process or value improvement.

Meanwhile, the data needed to guide decisions simply doesn't exist. Builders claim deep historical knowledge, yet no one in the supply chain—owners, designers, builders, or cost consultants—has captured reliable, contextual data tied to the real causes behind cost outcomes. Estimates are built from surface-level comps or SWAGs (scientific wild-ass guesses), more influenced by what the owner can tolerate than what labor and materials actually cost.

Why has nothing changed? Because there's no shared structure. No standard. No system.

No Standards = No Knowledge
The lack of structured data is the root problem. Without a common standard, even the most advanced software can't connect data across phases, roles, or systems.

Organizations like CSI (Uniformat 2010) and ASTM (E1557 – Uniformat II) have attempted to develop standard cost frameworks. But instead of alignment, they compete—offering inconsistent, incomplete, overlapping, and conflicting definitions.

A survey of 20 preconstruction professionals from 13 ENR Top 100 builders confirmed it:

  • Uniformat has potential to set consensus data standards to integrate and automate across processes and technologies.
  • But both CSI and ASTM versions are inconsistent, error-prone, and incomplete
  • Most issues could be resolved through a consensus-building effort across owners, designers, builders, and data professionals

Yet when asked to collaborate, none of the builders agreed to participate. Why?

  • Uniformat has potential to set consensus data standards to integrate and automate across processes and technologies.

Because any solution that creates transparency also threatens the estimator's role and builder's profit sources.

That brings us directly to Problem 4 – Cost-Based Compensation, the core driver behind construction's resistance to change.

Problem 4 – Cost-Based Compensation Drives Waste ×

A typical exam room built today in St. Louis costs about $200,000—nearly double what national inflation would justify. This isn't an anomaly—it's the result of a system where the entire supply chain profits from higher costs and more time.

The root cause? Cost-plus and billable-hour compensation structures.
From owner reps and architects to builders and subcontractors, everyone's profit increases when a project costs more and enables more hours to be billed.

This financial model is the common thread behind the other problems:

Many professionals—and their companies—are trapped in a growing conflict between their intrinsic motivation to serve clients and the financial incentives that push them toward short-term profit. The result? Trailer villages of billable workers on-site, generating work for one another in a highly manual process (see Problem 3), while keeping owner reps and facility staff constantly engaged with layers of "customer service." It's madness: cost-plus and billable-hour compensation reward inefficiency and busyness over value, fiscal responsibility, and meaningful outcomes.

A Broken Promise

Construction Management (CM) emerged in the 1970s to help owners control cost and schedule. More recently, Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) and other negotiated delivery models aimed to improve collaboration. But instead of increasing productivity, they've simply normalized inflated budgets and timelines.

Builders now "solve" cost and schedule issues by inflating the baseline—padding estimates with allowances and contingencies, often shaped more by what an owner can tolerate than by real material and labor costs.

Subcontractors follow suit, especially when brought on early as "trade partners." Architects, too, are financially motivated to overdesign. The result? A cooperative—but costly—project.

Facility managers often accept this because they're treated well. Claims and disputes are easier to manage under bloated budgets. But behind the customer service smiles, waste has become systemic.

Deming Warned Us

"If they remember me at all," Deming once said, "I hope it's as someone who tried to prevent America from committing suicide."

The construction industry is doing just that—burying value under bloated budgets, inflated designs, and misaligned incentives. With national debt surpassing $36 trillion, continuing down this path isn't just irresponsible—it's self-destructive.
