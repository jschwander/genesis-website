Published escalation data since 1967 shows that today's buildings cost more than twice what national inflation would justify. Some owners are paying nearly three times what they should. And there's no sign of the trend slowing.

Fragmented System

Problem 3 – Fragmented and Archaic Processes Nearly every process applied in today's facility planning, design, and construction stage is fragmented, one-off, and manual. In an age of next day delivery, self-checkout, mychart medical portal, and real-time Siri directions – planning and managing building programs couldn't be more archaic and lacking knowledge. As shown in Figure 1, the thirteen planning and pre-construction processes are completely siloed, requiring manual, file-based, transactions between owner and/or owner-rep, the AE team, and the builder. Figure 1 – Preconstruction Silos This siloed state exist, not only between the owner, designer, and builder, but internally as well. In the case of the builder, there's virtually no integration or automation between business development, preconstruction/estimating, procurement, project/contract management, accounting, or enterprise management. File-based Excel spreadsheets and biased opinions remain the go-to information resources at every step of facility planning, programming, budgeting, decision-making, and project management. Building Information Modeling (BIM), Virtual Design and Construction (VDC), Lean Construction, Integrated Project Delivery (IPD), Virtual Reality (VR), and myriad of other advanced technical solutions promised to make the design and construction process more efficient and effective. However, as we've seen in Data Discovery 2 – Waste – these have only added to the already excessive overhead and waste in time and cost. The implication of this state is remarkable causing the blind to lead the blind. The majority of owners, owner reps, designers, and lenders depend on the builder's to predict the construction costs on most of today's projects. Builders sell their vast historical data resources but they have virtually no contextual data from which to make this claim. Why? No builder has ever captured real world project data results, based on the causes that determine those results. Even the most sophisticated builder has no reliable data resource for budgeting or steering project outcomes. Today's estimators, like their grandfathers, can do little more than run some whole building comps, or even some anecdotal surface area-based assemblies, like roofing unit costs. But these comps are more dangerous than useful. At the end of the day, estimators resort to their best guess. That guess is far more influenced by what they believe the owner can tolerate than by what the materials and labor may costs. No Standards equals No Knowledge Surely, we can't blame estimators for this state. Two things would be needed for a reliable, contextual historical data system to exist: Data Structures/Standards and Data Processing. Without these construction remains in its archaic state. Since build projects are so complex so too, the standards and processing solutions must be sufficiently sophisticated to account for the myriads of interactions between causes and effects. The Construction Specifications Institute (CSI) and Advancing Standards of Transforming Markets (ASTM) have developed some standards. The problem is that they are competing, and fraught with errors, omissions, and inconsistencies. A recent survey 20 preconstruction and estimating professionals from 13 of Engineering News Record (ENR) Top 100 builders proved this claim. Here are the some of the key results of that survey: Result 1 Result 2 …Result N There's overwhelming recognition that construction standards are a mess. There is also significant agreement that if the standards mess could be solved, then the builders' C-suite would welcome and advocate for such standards. The survey and follow up meetings presented an easy path toward fixing this problem. Unfortunately, none of the preconstruction / estimating professionals were willing to take the next step of joining a collaboration effort with other AEC professionals. Why is this? That brings us to Problem No. 4 Cost-based Compensation. This problem must be solved before the other three can be. Fortunately, everything is in place to start solving all these problems.

Cost-Plus Contracting Fuels Waste

Problem 4 - Cost Plus A typical exam room built today in the Greater St. Louis region will cost, on average, $200,000. That's $100,000 more than what it should cost. Of the $200,000, the shows that more than $150,000 is expended on overhead, indirect and supporting labor, other waste, and profit generated from waste. This brings us to Problem 4 – Cost-plus and Billable-Hour based contracting. Owner's pay double because of the compensation of the entire supply chain, is based on cost and time. The more a project cost, and/or the more billable hours, the greater the profit. This situation applies to owner reps, cost consultants, architects, engineers, builders, and subcontractors. The problem has grown so massive, and the solution so ready, for anyone in this supply chain to flip the profit motive and start generating profit by reducing waste – starting on a new building project that's in the early planning or design stage. The Construction Management (CM) delivery method emerged in the early 1970's to solve the twin problems of projects coming in over budget and/or behind schedule. CM and, more recently Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) have solved these problems – not by increasing productivity or reducing uncertainty – but by shaping excessive budgets and delivery dates. The solution has created a far greater problem. Not only are owner's borrowing and paying more than double what they should, the aggregated impact on the economy and national debt is reaching suicidal proportions. This phenomenon presents one of history's greatest economic boondoggles, with no end in sight if we don't flip the financial motivation soon. Here's just one scenario that has various twists to it that accounts for the excessive and growing budgets and costs: Owner and/or Owner's Rep Solicits RFPs from multiple CMs – The RFP includes request for a budget estimate along with a fee proposal and labor charging rates. The CM's produce a wide range of detailed responses with an extensive list of assumptions, qualifications, and allowances. Owner Selects one of the CMs - It should make common sense that unconstrained cost-plus and/or billable-hour based contracting would would cost $x, if construction had escalated at the rate of national inflation since the early 1970's. Primarily, due to CM and other cost-plus and billable hour hour-basesd contracting, that classroom costs taxpayer $2x. On the other hand it creates one the greatest economic opportunities ever, for innovative designers and builders to turn waste into higher owner value with better buildings and/or lower cost while generating a substantially higher return-on-effort, with dramatically lower staffing needs. If, in 1970, non-productive, indirect labor, overhead, and profit amounted to 25% of the project – it has skyrocketed to 125% of the project.

A Proven Management Philosophy for Construction

Construction's cost problems are not caused by poor effort — they are systemic. W. Edwards Deming showed that to improve outcomes, industries must align incentives, base decisions on impartial data, organize processes around a shared truth, and eliminate fear from improvement. His philosophy transformed manufacturing and inspired the Toyota Production System, proving that systems thinking — not isolated fixes — leads to lasting gains.

W. Edwards Deming
"94% of problems in business are systems-driven and only 6% are people-driven."

Construction, with its complexity and fragmentation, stands to benefit even more by applying these same principles from the earliest stages of planning and design. Applying Deming's "system of profound knowledge" allows owners, consultants, designers, and builders to evaluate and improve value at every step, replacing outdated, wasteful compensation models with outcome-focused incentives.
