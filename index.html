The Industry is Stuck in a Time Warp

For over 60 years, construction has operated under outdated models. Cost-plus and billable-hour contracts reward builders for maximizing time and expense—not delivering results. The consequences are clear: U.S. construction productivity has stagnated since the 1940s, while costs have surged. Builders make more money by doing things the long way. Today's planning, design, and cost management processes remain fragmented, inefficient, and archaic—especially when compared to industries like manufacturing and retail. And while new technologies and "lean" initiatives promise progress, they often just add more layers of complexity and cost. The result? Buildings now cost nearly twice what material and labor inflation alone can explain.

"Since the 1940s, the rest of the economy has seen productivity grow by more than 8 times. But construction productivity has hardly improved. We build like we did 80 years ago. The result? Construction costs increasing by 5.38% annually—far outpacing both the 2.4% national inflation rate and 3.5% building materials costs." — McKinsey Global Institute
"Between 1997 and 2021, construction productivity actually fell by 7 percent, whereas in the manufacturing sector it rose by 126 percent." — KPMG

This Isn't New. It's Proven.

In the 1980s, Toyota forced a change in American manufacturing. The foundation? A systems-based philosophy created by W. Edwards Deming. Genesis applies that same philosophy to construction—finally.

1940s – World War II

Deming Introduces Statistical Quality Control

As U.S. industries scaled up for war, W. Edwards Deming introduced statistical process control (SPC) to reduce waste and improve quality. Mass production without mass defect.

1947

Deming Invited to Post-War Japan

General MacArthur's team invited Deming to help rebuild Japan's economy, introducing process-focused, long-term thinking. Fix the process, not the people.

1950-1960

Japan Acts on Deming's Vision

highlighting how Japan institutionalized Deming's teachings and developed practices like Kaizen and Just-in-Time manufacturing.

1980

NBC Documentary: "If Japan Can... Why Can't We?"

This program shocked Americans, revealing Japan's manufacturing superiority and crediting Deming, triggering U.S. interest. Deming re-enters U.S. spotlight.

1986

Toyota Sets the Standard

Toyota's system, built on Deming's principles, becomes a global model of quality. Continuous improvement revolutionizes manufacturing.

1999

Genesis is Founded

Created as a pilot for Steelcase and Workstage, Genesis brings Deming's system to construction. Applying proven methods to a new sector.
